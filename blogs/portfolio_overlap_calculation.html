Consider a scenario, where you already have a portfolio of ₹24,000 and would like to invest ₹12,000 in a new fund. From the table above, you can see the how the total amount is spread across different securities based on its weights in the portfolio.

When we say, overlap, it can be calculated from two points of view.

Case 1:

Consider the case when you would like to invest in a new fund. You would like to know how much amount from the new fund goes to the securities which are part of the existing portfolio. To calculate overlap, we first get the common fund, which is minimum of the amount in the portfolio and in the new fund for each security.

The overlap percentage of the new fund with the portfolio is reported as follows:

OverlapPercentage=CommonFund/FundAmount=C/BOverlap Percentage = Common Fund / Fund Amount = C/BOverlapPercentage=CommonFund/FundAmount=C/B﻿

In this case, ₹8,000 out of ₹12,000 goes to the securities that are part of the portfolio, which equates to 67%.

Case 2:

You have already invested in multiple fund and would now like to see what all Funds are overlapping and are not contributing for diversification.

As the “New fund” is already part of the portfolio, 2*₹8,000 = ₹16,000 out of the total of ₹36,000 would be invested in the same securities. Overlap percentage is reported as follows:

OverlapPercentage=2∗CommonFund/TotalPortfolioAmount=2∗C/(A+B)Overlap Percentage = 2*Common Fund / Total Portfolio Amount = 2*C/(A+B)OverlapPercentage=2∗CommonFund/TotalPortfolioAmount=2∗C/(A+B)﻿

In this case it is 45%.
